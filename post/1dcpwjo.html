Here are the five teams who came back from a 2-0 NBA Finals deficit

Way back in 2021, the Milwaukee Bucks trailed 2-0 to Phoenix after dropping the first two out west. After a neck and neck opening to the third game in MKE, a 30-9 run to end the first half kept the Bucks comfortably ahead for much of the rest of the game. They rallied in the fourth to win Game Four, were spurred on by that play in Game Five, and won the title with Giannis’s 50 back home.

Way way back in 2016, the Cleveland Cavaliers not only trailed 2-0, but went down three games to one behind three double digit losses to a 73-9 Warriors team who famously were so good they rolled to the 2017 title adding only a random Twitter user. The Cavs, back in Oakland to face a Golden State team without forward/recidivist Draymond Green, rallied in Game Five behind 41 points each from LeBron James and Boooooooooo, then won back home in Cleveland with another 41 from LeBron, and eked out a tense, back and forth Game Seven spurred on by that play. Or was it that play?

Way way way back in 2006, the Miami Heat looked a step behind against Dirk Nowitzki and the Dallas Mavericks (hey!), basically out of commission from the second quarter of Game One all the way through the flight out of Dallas. Back home in Miami, they rallied from a 13 point fourth quarter deficit on the strength of not just that play from Gary Payton, but 42 from Dwyane Wade, followed by 36 from Dwyane Wade, and then 25 (free throws) from Dwyane Wade to head back to Texas up three games to two. Pat Riley famously both instructed his team to pack for one day and spent much of the second half yelling at them in timeouts to simply be man enough to win, and it paid off, with a 95-92 win to clinch their first NBA championship.

Way way way way back in 1977, the Portland Trailblazers were marked underdogs vs. the Philadelphia 76ers, led by new arrivals Dr. J and George McGinnis. Local hippie Bill Walton had led the team in scoring in both of the first two games, both losses back East. He stayed by the basket and moved into defense and distribution, leading all players in rebounds for each of the next four games, as Portland won two blowouts at the Coliseum, snuck past Philly with a monster third quarter in Game Five, and, sparked by those plays in Game Six, Big Red delivered Portland its first title and only one until they famously drafted Naismith winner and Olympic Gold Medalist Michael Jordan with the number two pick in 1984. RIP, Bill.

Way way way way way back in 1969, the Boston Celtics (hell yeah), fourth seeded in the East, trailed two games to none to the powerful Los Angeles Lakers, well known for winning many titles in Tinseltown, aka Minneapolis, California. Jerry West scored 94 points in the first two games, on the way to a Finals MVP and a genuinely unfair career in the playoffs. But led by player coach and Hall of Fame Human Bill Russell, the Celtics clawed back two in the fourth quarters of Game Three and Game Four, winning on a Bailey Howard buzzer beater to even the series. After another loss in LA in Game Five (26 from West and Elgin Baylor, 18 boards from Wilt Chamberlain), the Celtics jumped out early in a rough on the eyes Game Six, and, sparked by the display of balloons on the Forum ceiling and the USC marching band in the hallway, held a 15 point fourth quarter lead in Game Seven. After Wilt Chamberlain limped off with five minutes left, Coach Butch van Breda Kolff infamously kept Wilt out of the game as he felt better and the Lakers rallied. Late in the game, as the shot clock wound down, Don Nelson made that play, and the Celtics held on for a two point victory and their 11th NBA title of the Russell era.
