Best Fits for the Remaining Free Agents

With salaries exploding around the NBA, players should be throwing a party. But unfortunately, that party also includes a game of musical chairs where a few get left out of the cap space parade. For them, it may make sense to lick their wounds and return to the market next year.

Here are the most intriguing among them –

(1) PG Tyus Jones, Washington, 28 years old

It’s a new era in the NBA, one where traditional point guards with traditional size are being devalued, as illustrated by the lack of a market for Tyus Jones. He’s a throwback at the PG spot – a steady eddie field general. Limited by his athleticism and his size (at 6’1”), he’s always been viewed as a high-end backup. Still, Jones is a smart player who has always been productive. Per 36, he’s averaged 13.0 points, 7.6 assists, 1.8 steals, and only 1.2 TO, one of the best A/TO ratios in the entire league. He’s somewhere between a poor man’s Mike Conley and a rich man’s Monte Morris.

best fit: Presuming Tyus Jones and his team can’t work out a better sign-and-trade for $10M+ somewhere, he may have to take a one-year deal to return to the market next year. The best place for him to stay in the spotlight may be Denver. They’ve lacked a backup PG since Monte Morris left. Frankly, they lack depth across the board; their roster goes about as deep as a microphallus. Not only could Tyus Jones anchor a bench unit, he’d buffer them from a potentially-disasterous scenario if Jamal Murray or Nikola Jokic misses extended time.

(2) SG Gary Trent Jr., Toronto, 26 years old

I thought that Gary Trent Jr. would sign a healthy $60M contract given his theoretical role as a 3+D wing, but the market has spoken and suggested that they don’t buy his act. His defensive effort has clearly waned since a plucky run with Portland in the bubble. The shooting is still there, though, as he hit 39.3% from deep last year and averages 38.6% for his career. He’s not going to give you much else (with meek averages of 3.0 rebounds and 1.9 assists per 36 in his career), but that’s still something.

best fit: If Trent Jr. needs to wait a year to re-enter free agency, then re-uniting with Damian Lillard in Milwaukee makes the most sense from both sides. Like Denver, the Bucks’ depth has been a problem for them, particularly on the wing spots. Trent Jr. isn’t a world beater, but he’s probably better than departing Malik Beasley or Pat Connaughton or the young prospects (MarJon Beauchamp and Andre Jackson Jr.) that Doc Rivers will refuse to develop anyway.

(3) SG Luke Kennard, Memphis, 28 years old

Luke Kennard isn’t just a “shooter” – he’s a flamethrower. He’s legitimately one of the best bombers in the league, with career averages of 43.9% from three and 87.9% from the line. Heck, he’s averaged over 45.0% from three across the last three seasons combined. Obviously, there’s a reason Kennard isn’t raking in huge money. He’s injury prone, and limited by his size (6’5”, 6’5” wingspan). As a result, teams have been reluctant to trust him with a major role. He’s only started 110 games across 7 his seasons in the league.

best fit: It sounds like Luke Kennard is working toward re-signing with Memphis, but I’ve never particularly liked his fit there. After all, the Grizzlies have a similar (but better) version of him with Desmond Bane, capping his upside. He’d be best served on a team with bigger perimeter players who can cover for him defensively, or perhaps a defensive eraser like Victor Wembanyama in San Antonio. Ever since the Spurs perfected “the Beautiful Game” and beat the Heatles in the Finals, they’ve ranked in the bottom 10 in three point attempts.

other names of note

Is it really the end of the road of relevance for former # 1 pick Markelle Fultz? Maybe. He still can’t stay healthy or hit (or even attempt) a three. Perhaps he can utilize his length and reinvent himself as a Delon Wright-type backup. Landry Shamet is a more reliable combo guard who gives you some 3+D. At 6’8”, SF Jordan Nwora also has some of that potential at the wing spot.
