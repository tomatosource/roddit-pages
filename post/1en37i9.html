Why the Jazz didn’t trade Lauri, and why Lauri doesn’t “ruin the tank”

Unless you haven’t been on social media recently, you’ve likely seen that the Utah Jazz just inked 27yo MIP Lauri Markkanen to a 5-year, $238 million deal. With how much attention he’s gotten from media circuits and rumor mills over the past month or so, there’s been a lot of discourse about this move and what it means for the direction of the franchise. I’m a lifelong Jazz fan, and over the course of the last year, I’ve really taken to this iteration of the team, and Lauri specifically. Out of the 55 games that he played for our team last season, I probably watched upwards of 40 in their entirety. I went to games with my dad, bought his jersey, actually got my wife to watch sports, and overall had a really great time rooting for him and the team, lumps and all. I care about this team, and don’t look forward to the day when Lauri doesn’t suit up for the Jazz anymore.

¬†I also happen to be something of an obsessive, chronically online loser that cares way too much about tall guys putting a ball in a basket. As such, I’ve followed and thought about the Jazz discourse over the last month quite a bit. Now that the dust has settled a bit, the most common take among the greater NBA community seems to be something like this:

¬†”Is Danny Ainge stupid? With this signing, they’ve ruined their tank–doomed to not be good enough to contend, but also not be bad enough to have a chance at Cooper Flagg. Lauri’s a good player, but why would the Jazz do this when they could have traded him for 4 FRPs and bottomed out for a stacked 2025 draft? He’s not gonna be the guy that wills this franchise to a championship–the Jazz need their number one option, and he’s not it. Enjoy the 10th pick, I guess!”

¬†If you don’t really follow the Jazz, I get why you buy this. But the more I think about it, the more convinced I become that this is a lazy narrative that doesn’t give consideration to the nuances and difficulties of managing an NBA roster and building a championship contender.

¬†There are a lot of true statements here: the Jazz don’t have their number one guy, they need that guy to have a shot at a championship, and they aren’t maximizing their chances of drafting him. Lauri will win the Jazz games that they otherwise would lose. The heart of the issue here is lottery odds. There have been volumes upon volumes written about how to build a championship NBA team, and everyone seems to have a different opinion on how to get the job done that they’re very confident in.

¬†I submit to you that when all is said and done, winning an NBA championship is extremely simple: get lucky. There is no guarantee, no “capturing the Flagg”. There’s just getting lucky. Are the Jazz going to get Cooper Flagg? Probably not. But you know what, Brooklyn Nets fans, I mean no disrespect here, but you probably aren’t going to get him either. Pistons fans, you’re probably not going to get him. Blazers fans, you’re probably not going to get him. Someone is going to get him, but whoever you are, it’s probably not going to be you. If you do get him, he might be everything you’ve ever hoped for and alter the trajectory of your franchise. He also might not. Personally, I think he’s going to be a fantastic player, but as fans of the 2017-2018 Celtics are all too aware of, fantastic players aren‚Äôt immune to breaking their legs 5 minutes into the season.

¬†All of that said, there are, of course, things that you can do to maximize your odds of landing a great pick. But blindly doing everything possible to maximize your chances of winning big without considering the potential costs and benefits of your decisions is a dangerous game to play. Coming back around to Lauri, let’s take a shot at the costs and benefits of having him on the roster:

¬†As of right now, with Lauri Markkanen locked up on a long-term deal, the FanDuel sportsbook has the Jazz’s regular season win O/U at 27.5. That’s tied with the Bulls for the 5th worst O/U in the league (currently ranked above Detroit, Washington, Portland, and Brooklyn). When you dive into the weeds, this makes a lot of sense. The Jazz’s 23-24 record of 31-51 is quite deceiving. After selling on two key pieces at the trade deadline, Kelly Olynyk and Simone Fontecchio, the Jazz had the worst post-deadline record in the NBA: 5-25. Before you point out that they shut Lauri down to tank, Lauri played in 13 of those games. Guess what our record was? 1-12.

¬†So let’s say we sell Lauri for a pick-based package. We’re already pretty bad with him, but how much worse are we without him? It’s impossible to truly quantify, but there are advanced statistics that can at least give us a reasonable estimate. According to Basketball Reference, Lauri had 6.2 win shares in the 23-24 season. According to the DunksAndThrees EPM database, he came in at 8.3 estimated wins for the season. I’m not suggesting in any way that this is a perfect extrapolation, but let’s split the difference and say that Lauri is worth 7 wins. Let’s say that a replacement-level player worth 1 win (the NBA median according to DunksAndThrees) comes back as salary filler, and the Jazz have replaced 7 wins with 1. That would move our projected O/U down 6 wins, to 21.5. Where does that get us? Well, actually not very far. We leapfrog Detroit (25.5) and are tied with Portland for the 3rd worst O/U in the league. Factoring in coin flips for breaking the tie between 5⁄6 and 3⁄4, the Jazz would go from a 9.75% chance at Flagg to a 13.25% chance–a 3.5% increase.

So the question that the Jazz front office faces is this: would you rather have Lauri Markkanen and a 9.75% chance at Flagg, or a 13.25% chance at Flagg and a cache of draft picks that are quite unlikely to produce another player of Lauri’s caliber?

¬†If you picked the second option, I’m not going to point the finger and say you’re a dumbass. You’re just more risk-tolerant than I am.

¬†Here’s a quick thought experiment to solidify the principle: Let’s say that I knock on your door with a choice. You either get one lottery ticket that has a 15% chance of winning \(1,000, or you get a crisp \)100 bill. Be completely honest with yourself, what would you choose? In spite of the fact that the expected value of the lottery ticket (\(150) exceeds the value of \)100, I would bet that at least 90% of people take the \(100. Why? For most people, that cost just isn't worth the uncertainty. Now, if it's a choice between the same lottery ticket and \)10, that’s a different story. The uncertainty is the same, but the opportunity cost is much lower.

¬†Much like you probably wouldn’t point a finger at someone taking the \(100 and say "man, what a dumbass! They just missed out on a chance at \)1,000!“, I personally wouldn’t point a finger at the Jazz FO for saying that the cost of maximizing their lottery odds is just too high.

¬†I’m not writing this novel purely in the defense of a single Utah Jazz FO decision. I’m writing this because I think that it’s helpful to be reminded as fans that managing an NBA franchise is hard. It’s not as simple as flipping the “contention/tanking” switch. You can do everything right, but without luck on your side, you can still come up short. League executives shouldn’t be immune to our criticism, but I do think that we can give a little bit more grace. And when we do criticize a move (or a lack of a move), we should at least recognize that we are missing a gargantuan amount of relevant information.

¬†If you read all of this, thanks for humoring me.
