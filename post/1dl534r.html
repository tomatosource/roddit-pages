Isn’t it ironic? Don’t ya think? Will Hardy’s success as a coach may have hurt his coaching career

Will Hardy is the coach of the Utah Jazz. He’s a bright, successful guy. And that may be a problem. In fact, it’s hurt him in two specific ways.

part one: climbing the ladder like Spider-Man

Hardy rocketed up the charts as a rising star assistant, developed by Gregg Popovich and the San Antonio Spurs before spending a year as a top assistant in Boston. For him, it wasn’t a matter of “if” he’d get a head coaching job, it was a matter of “when.”

That happened when he was just 34 years old, tapped by Danny Ainge and the Utah Jazz to lead their new era.

The timing may have been poor from Hardy’s perspective though. Just a short time later, Boston head coach Ime Udoka’s personal issues blew up and led to his dismissal.

As alluded to on the NBA Finals broadcast, the interim job would have almost certainly been Hardy’s if he was still on staff. At the time, he was more experienced and highly-acclaimed than “second row” Joe Mazzulla.

If Hardy had taken the Boston job, there’s no telling how the team would have turned out. He may have won the title in his first year. He may NOT have won this year. Who knows. But there’s no doubt that he’d have had an opportunity to coach an excellent roster. In a sense, Hardy’s rapid ascent (and hire elsewhere) actually hurt him.

—-

part two: the floor is lava

Instead of coach a veteran team, Will Hardy took over a rebuilding Utah Jazz club.

At the time, the team was in full tear-down mode. They had just traded stars Donovan Mitchell and Rudy Gobert, preparing for a long cold winter.

Vegas set their over/under at 23.5, among the lowest that season. Of course, there was a major silver lining there. If the team was truly that bad, they’d rack up a lot of lottery balls and have the chance to draft a generational talent in Victor Wembanyama.

That didn’t happen. Thanks to Will Hardy’s coaching and the better-than-expected play of Lauri Markkanen and others, the Jazz weren’t half bad. They even flirted with the playoffs before settling in at 37-45. Hardy got a lot of praise for his coaching acumen and his overachievement.

But then again, his success may have hurt him. Being 37-45 is essentially like being in NBA purgatory. You’re not good enough to make the playoffs, not BAD enough to draft a stud like Wemby (who would go to his mentor Pop’s team, oddly enough.)

Because Hardy’s team never bottomed out, they never had the chance to draft a cornerstone top 5 pick. As a result of that, the rebuild is going to be more difficult. And once again, the problem may be because Hardy was too good.
